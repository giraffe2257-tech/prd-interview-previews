Section 1

1. 研究背景與目的

PRD(Product Requirements Document)是跨功能團隊在日常產品開發中最頻繁依賴的核心文件之一,同時承擔需求說明、跨角色合約、規格依據、會議錨點等多重功能。然而,作為一份「需要同時被不同角色閱讀、解讀、轉化」的文件,PRD 在實務中經常出現幾種反覆發生的現象:

  • 同一份文件被不同角色讀出不同重點,事後才發現理解落差
  • 寫得太細反而稀釋重點,寫得太模糊又容易造成重工或上線後問題
  • PRD 作為跨角色協作的起點,於實作過程中被轉譯為多份派生文件(Figma、Tech Spec、工單、Test Case 等);如何維繫從起點到實作端的一致性,是值得探討的議題

本研究希望透過實務工作者的第一手經驗,理解 PRD 在真實工作流程中如何被撰寫、閱讀、轉譯,並辨識哪些溝通痛點反覆出現、哪些已有有效的因應方式、哪些可能適合以工具或 AI 介入。研究成果將構成 King's College London MSc 學位論文的核心資料,並可能延伸為後續學術發表或工具設計提案。

Section 2

2. 為什麼邀請 PM 參與

一份 PRD 在組織中通常由「產出端」與「接收端」共同建構,其中產出端指 PRD 的撰寫者,多為產品經理(PM);接收端則指 PRD 的下游讀者與執行者,包含設計師、工程師、QA、Data Analyst 等。

「文件被寫出來時,作者腦中假設了哪些讀者、為哪些段落保留了什麼、又有意或無意省略了什麼。」

由於研究者本身的職涯方向亦將往 PM 邁進,且產品開發流程在實務上多以 PM 發想需求為起點,本研究遂以 PM 視角作為核心切入點,希望從上述這個源頭出發,理解 PRD 在後續流轉中所遇到的各種現象。PM 受訪者的第一手經驗(包括撰寫策略、判斷標準、與下游角色之間的溝通方式),構成本研究最關鍵的核心資料。

Feature 01 · For PM Participants

Product Manager

The study examines the readers a PRD author silently assumes, what is preserved in each section, and what is intentionally or unintentionally omitted. The PM perspective is the core entry point of this research.

Duration
60–75 min

Format
Semi-structured
Location
Online or in person
Section 1

1. Research Background and Aim

The Product Requirements Document (PRD) is one of the most frequently relied-upon documents in day-to-day cross-functional product development. As a document that is "read, interpreted, and transformed by different roles at the same time", PRDs in practice repeatedly produce several recognisable patterns:

  • The same document is read differently by different roles, with discrepancies in understanding only surfacing later
  • Writing in too much detail dilutes the key points, while writing too ambiguously causes rework or post-launch issues
  • As the starting point of cross-role collaboration, a PRD is translated during implementation into multiple derivative documents (Figma, Tech Spec, tickets, Test Cases, etc.); maintaining consistency from origin to implementation is a question worth exploring

Drawing on first-hand accounts from practitioners, this study seeks to understand how PRDs are written, read, and translated in real workflows, and to identify which communication pain points recur, which coping strategies prove effective, and which might be supported by tools or AI. The findings will form the core data of a King's College London MSc dissertation, with possible extension into academic publication or tool design proposals.

Section 2

2. Why PMs Are Invited

A PRD is typically co-constructed by a producing side (the writer, usually the PM) and a receiving side (downstream readers and executors, including designers, engineers, QA, and Data Analysts).

"When a document is written, what readers does the author silently assume, what is preserved in each section, and what is intentionally or unintentionally omitted?"

As the researcher's own career trajectory is moving towards PM, and product development workflows typically begin with PM ideation, this study takes the PM perspective as its core entry point, starting from this source to understand the phenomena PRDs encounter in their subsequent flow. PM participants' first-hand experience (writing strategies, judgment criteria, communication with downstream roles) forms the most critical core data of this study.

Interview Guide · Section 3

3. Warm-Up

Before moving into the main topics, the participant will be invited to briefly share (about 5 minutes):

  1. Participant background: current company or industry, current position, and time in this position
  2. Team's product development flow: roughly what the team's day-to-day workflow looks like
Section 4

4. Topic Areas

The items below are topic directions, not a list of questions to be asked one by one. When an answer already covers another question, it will simply be skipped.

The PRD writing flow (the largest block, ~25–30 min)

The participant will be invited to walk the researcher through the most recent PRD they wrote, end to end — from receiving the requirement, deciding on a writing strategy, to handing off to design and engineering. The more specific the better:

  • What files are opened during the writing process? Which steps tend to get most stuck?
  • What content is explicitly written into the PRD? What is left to "team tacit knowledge", "verbal additions in meetings", or "expected readers will fill in themselves"?
  • How is the threshold of "enough has been written" judged? Is there an implicit checklist?
  • For sections aimed at "engineer readers" versus "designer readers", is the writing strategy consciously differentiated?

Pain points, tools, and the role of AI

  • What is the issue that recurs most often in cross-role communication?
  • Has there ever been an experience where "an ambiguous PRD led to rework, conflict, or delay"? How was that gap later filled?
  • For the "expected outcomes / metrics" stated in a PRD, who tracks them post-launch, and who reports back? Do these reports feed back into the PRD? Has this cross-role accountability ever been a pain point?
  • What workarounds has the team developed? Which actually work, and which are only "done for form's sake"?
  • Have any AI tools been used in PRD-related work? Which part of the flow? (No experience is fine — the topic can simply be skipped.)
  • Imagine a tool dedicated to helping the readers of PRDs (rather than the writers): what should it ideally look like, and what should it definitely not do?
Section 5

5. Closing Questions

  1. Anything not covered today that feels important for this research topic?
  2. Would there be willingness to share an anonymised PRD for document analysis? (A second de-identification pass will be applied upon receipt.)
  3. Are there colleagues who might be suitable as the next interviewees? (PM, Designer, Engineer, QA, DA are all welcome.)
Section 6

6. Format Notes

  • There is no right answer. The study cares about real working experience — negative experiences, complaints, and unfinished thoughts are all valuable material; no polishing is needed.
  • Any question can be declined at any time; the interview can be paused or stopped at any point.
  • If something is clearer to draw than to describe (flowcharts, organisational relationships, document mappings…), pen and paper or a whiteboard will be available on the spot.
  • If convenient, having a recently written PRD open during the interview is useful as a memory anchor; it does not need to be shared, but it helps make "the most recent PRD I wrote" more concrete.
  • Data can be withdrawn within 30 days of the interview, without giving a reason.
Section 7 · Optional

7. Phase Two

After common patterns are identified from the first phase, a proposed improvement will be developed, and participants will be invited back for one feedback interview (about 45–60 minutes). Whether to participate in the second phase can be decided after the first-phase interview is complete.

For any questions about this document, or anything that would be useful to clarify in advance, please feel free to reply. Many thanks for taking the time.

1. Research Background and Aim

A PRD is typically co-constructed by a "producing side" (the writer, usually the PM) and a "receiving side" (downstream readers and executors, including designers, engineers, QA, and Data Analysts). The "engineer" role in practice covers both frontend and backend; the two face PRDs and collaborate with other roles in noticeably different ways.

"How design intent becomes the actual system behaviour layered into code, runtime configuration, and the deployed version."

The backend engineer's perspective offers a view of this transformation. In addition, backend work frequently involves sharing APIs across teams and handling schema changes — situations the PM often cannot see, leading to cases where scenarios described in the PRD cannot actually be carried out. These situations are also a focus of this study.

Section 1

1. Research Background and Aim

The Product Requirements Document (PRD) is one of the most frequently relied-upon documents in day-to-day cross-functional product development. As a document that is "read, interpreted, and transformed by different roles at the same time", PRDs in practice repeatedly produce several recognisable patterns:

  • The same document is read differently by different roles, with discrepancies in understanding only surfacing later
  • Writing in too much detail dilutes the key points, while writing too ambiguously causes rework or post-launch issues
  • As the starting point of cross-role collaboration, a PRD is translated during implementation into multiple derivative documents
Section 2

2. Why This Hybrid Profile Is Invited

For this study, a participant who simultaneously holds engineer and Tech PM roles is an especially valuable observation subject, for two reasons:

  1. The same individual carries an internal cross-role perspective. Interviews with pure PMs or pure RDs can only show one side at a time, while a hybrid participant can directly describe the shift in thinking when switching between the receiving and producing sides.
  2. The unique features of the iOS (mobile) platform. The derivative document chain (often centred on Figma), release flow (App Store review, backwards compatibility), and SSOT structure (code + App Store version + remote config / feature flag) all have distinctive characteristics.

Given that the participant's working time is roughly split iOS engineer 70% / Tech PM 30%, the interview content will broadly follow this proportion: iOS RD perspective gets the largest weight, with Tech PM perspective as the second axis, followed by pain points and the iOS post-implementation flow.

4. Topic Areas

The role split is roughly iOS RD 70% / Tech PM 30%, and interview time will be allocated accordingly.

iOS RD perspective: reading PRDs and downstream artefacts (the largest block, ~20–25 min)

The participant will first be invited, as an iOS engineer, to walk the researcher through the most recent end-to-end workflow after receiving a PRD:

  • What is the first action taken upon receiving a PRD? Read top to bottom? Jump straight to acceptance criteria? Find someone to ask first?
  • Which step tends to get most stuck?
  • What content isn't written in the PRD but has to be decided independently?
  • Which sections are deliberately skipped? Has skipping them ever caused regret?
  • How is "understood enough to break down tickets and estimate work" judged?
  • What artefacts does a PRD typically get translated into? Is the translation done individually, or as a team?
  • Are there parts of the PRD that are "not really understood, but usually not asked about either"?
  • In an iOS development context, derivative documents often centre on Figma; between receiving a PRD and starting to write code, how many "intermediate documents" are typically produced? Which one is actually relied on?
  • When different documents contradict each other, which one is treated as authoritative?
  • On mobile, where is the real answer to "what is the system actually doing right now"? In the code base, or in code + the current App Store version (including older versions still running on user devices) + remote config / feature flag + iOS version / device compatibility taken together?

Tech PM perspective: writing PRDs and cross-role collaboration (~10–15 min)

  • Under what conditions does the role switch from iOS engineer to Tech PM? What are the differences in thinking and decision-making pace?
  • When writing PRDs as Tech PM, what files are opened? Which steps tend to get most stuck?
  • What content is explicitly written into the PRD? What is left to "team tacit knowledge", "verbal additions in meetings", or "expected readers will fill in themselves"?
  • Given an iOS engineering background, does writing for engineers tend to be more concrete, or more comfortable with omission?
  • Holding both engineer and PM identities, do trade-offs in PRD writing differ from a "pure PM perspective"? Did those trade-offs prove to be strengths or limitations?
  • Has there ever been an experience of writing a PRD as Tech PM, then receiving the same PRD as iOS RD (or vice versa)? What insights did this "self-receiving" experience produce?

Pain points, tools, and AI

  • What is the issue that recurs most often in cross-role communication?
  • Has there ever been an experience where "an ambiguous PRD led to rework, conflict, or delay"?
  • The most difficult edge case ever encountered: did it come from genuinely complex business logic, or from spilling into another team's scope?
  • What workarounds has the team developed? Which actually work, and which are only "done for form's sake"?
  • Have any AI tools been used in PRD-related work? (Including reading PRDs as RD, and writing PRDs as Tech PM.)
  • Imagine a tool dedicated to helping the readers of PRDs: what should it ideally look like, and what should it definitely not do?
  • When PRDs, Figma, and Test Cases need to be integrated into "what engineers actually rely on", who should do that integration work?

iOS post-implementation flow and quality control (mobile-specific)

  • Code review / PR flow: Does code review surface inconsistencies with the PRD? Does iOS use any specific review or CI tooling (Bitrise, Fastlane lane design, etc.)?
  • Release flow: TestFlight beta? Phased release? Are server-side feature flags / remote config used to gate new features? How is App Store review time factored into project scheduling?
  • Backwards compatibility: What is the team's iOS deployment target? What is the workflow for deprecated APIs and crashes on older versions?
  • Post-launch monitoring: Crash rate, adoption rate, event tracking — who designs and who tracks them?
  • Incident response: What is the coordination flow when a hotfix needs expedited App Store review? Do post-mortems feed back into how PRDs are written?
  • Cross-team / cross-platform collaboration: Where are the API specifications shared between iOS, backend, Android, and web documented? When the same feature differs in iOS / Android / web implementations, how is it discussed and recorded?

Section 5

05 對未來角色的預測 — make it work / make it better

受訪者反轉了訪談者預設的問題（「AI 能不能讓 PM 寫更好的 PRD？」）：

我覺得不是把 PRD 寫得更詳細——可能是 PM 以後不需要 engineer 了。如果他們有 idea，可以先自己寫一個 POC，發給用戶用，回收數據；如果結果好，再把 idea 發給 engineer 做 scale up。 — Engineer-02 · 對 PM 角色的大膽預測

Stage A · 0 → 1

"Make it work"

把一個粗糙的功能先做出來，丟給用戶試試水溫。重點是快，不是好。

  • 不需要 review meeting、不需要 Tech Spec
  • 單一人推到底，不需要溝通成本
  • 失敗了損失也小
PM + AI vibe coding

Stage B · 1 → N

"Make it better"

已有 POC + 用戶數據驗證後，engineer 接手做穩定性、效能、規模化。

  • 正式 PRD + 跨團隊 review
  • Tech Spec + Tech Lead OK
  • QA / 監控 / hot-fix 全套
Engineer + 既有流程

受訪者自評：在 TikTok 工作 ~80% 是 "make it better"。所以即便 "make it work" 改由 PM + AI 完成，結構也不會被全面改寫——但漏斗的源頭會被加速。

現在我們做 engineer 的，我們的 value 是來說「這個 problem 值不值得 solve / it's a beautiful problem to solve」。 — Engineer-02 · 工程師價值的位移

另一個角色被打到 — DA：以前工程師寫 SQL 把 metric 拉給 PM；現在 PM 自己用 AI 寫 SQL，DA 角色被部分取代。 → 此觀察是 §III Synthesis · Diagram 2（AI 輔助流程） 的核心動力之一。
